CHAPTER 4
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

This chapter presents the results of the development, implementation, and evaluation of LenLearn: A Secure LMS with Monitoring Records and AI-Powered Plagiarism Checker for Glendale School using Browser-Based Restriction. The findings are organized in accordance with the procedures and evaluation methods described in Chapter 3 and are examined in relation to the objectives of the study, applicable standards—including OWASP ASVS, NIST account-security guidance, ISO 27001-oriented access and logging controls, and the FURPS quality model—and relevant literature on secure learning management systems, academic integrity tools, and browser-based assessment proctoring.

The chapter is divided into two major parts. Section 4.1 presents the system implementation overview, including the deployed administrator, faculty, and student modules illustrated in Figures 4.2 through 4.5. Sections 4.2 through 4.4 present the results of FURPS-based quality evaluation, STRIDE-oriented security testing and vulnerability assessment, and AI module performance. Section 4.5 discusses the meaning of these findings, their alignment with the study objectives, and their practical, technical, and academic significance. Limitations noted during data collection in Section 3.8.4 are considered throughout the interpretation.

4.1 System Implementation Overview

LenLearn: A Secure LMS with Monitoring Records and AI-Powered Plagiarism Checker for Glendale School using Browser-Based Restriction was developed based on the requirements identified in the earlier phases of the study. The system was designed to support digital learning management, apply cybersecurity controls, and provide academic integrity monitoring through role-based administrator, faculty, and student portals.

The implemented system includes integrated modules for user management, curriculum and subject administration, quiz and gradebook management, AI-powered originality checking, monitoring records with audit trail generation, browser-based quiz restriction, and data backup with archive vault storage. Each module was developed to address functional and security requirements identified during the client consultation with Glendale High School, the problem analysis, and the system design stages in Chapter 3.

The institute governance module allows authorized administrators to create, update, and manage student and faculty records, sections, subjects, curriculum guides, and school announcements.

The monitoring records module provides administrators with visibility into security-relevant system activity. Audit logs and LMS activity records may include login events, account lockouts, unauthorized access attempts, grade modifications, backup operations, and related actions that support accountability and traceability.

Figure 4.5. Faculty quiz roster at glendalehs-lms.com displaying tab-switch violation indicators for secured quiz submissions.

The faculty portal supports quiz configuration with timed assessments and lockdown settings, gradebook management with audited score changes, study material uploads, and submission review including quiz violation indicators. The student portal provides access to enrolled subjects, curriculum guides, assignments, quizzes, and grades, with terms-and-conditions enforcement and idle-session logout after thirty minutes of inactivity. Across all portals, LenLearn applies shared security controls that include parameterized PostgreSQL queries, input sanitization middleware, AES-256-GCM encryption for selected student personally identifiable information fields, Helmet HTTP security headers, strict CORS policies, and tiered API rate limiting.

The system was deployed in a controlled web-based testing environment for evaluation. A production instance was hosted on Railway with Cloudflare edge protection and PostgreSQL database services at glendalehs-lms.com. Local development and authorized security testing were conducted on localhost environments as described in Chapter 3. These environments were used to evaluate system functionality, performance, reliability, and security through FURPS assessment, STRIDE-based vulnerability testing, and controlled demonstration sessions with Glendale High School participants prior to any full-scale institutional adoption.

Overall, the combined security weighted mean of 4.08 (Agree) indicates that security features were generally perceived as present and appropriate, though session-management items were rated more modestly than access-control and data-privacy items; these perceptions should be read alongside the STRIDE-based test results in Section 4.3 rather than as standalone proof of security.

The overall success rate for executed tests was 88.0%, computed as (22 ÷ 25) × 100, excluding the not-applicable concurrent-session case. A separate SQL injection assessment comprising five white-box test cases achieved a 100% pass rate with zero vulnerabilities identified. Table 4.7 summarizes domain-level outcomes.

4.3.1 Spoofing

Spoofing threats—impersonating a legitimate user or forging identity—were tested through authentication controls and portal-role enforcement. All five authentication test cases passed. Invalid credentials were rejected with generic error messages that do not distinguish between unknown usernames and incorrect passwords (AUTH-002), mitigating user enumeration. Direct navigation to protected administrator, faculty, and student routes without an active session redirected to the appropriate login page, and unauthenticated API requests to protected endpoints returned HTTP 401 (AUTH-003). Account lockout activated after five consecutive failed attempts with a five-minute cooldown, and lockout events were written to LMS activity logs (AUTH-001). Role-based login portals blocked student credentials from faculty login and vice versa (AUTH-005).

Two-factor authentication via email OTP adds a second verification factor for accounts with 2FA enabled, reducing the impact of stolen passwords. Rate limiting on sign-in (ten attempts per fifteen minutes), password reset (three per hour), and OTP endpoints further constrains online guessing. Automated backend evidence confirmed SameSite session cookies, HttpOnly flags, and strict CORS origin allowlists as complementary anti-spoofing and anti-CSRF measures. Residual spoofing risk remains if a valid session cookie is exfiltrated from an compromised endpoint device; HttpOnly cookies and absence of password storage in browser localStorage reduce but do not eliminate this exposure.

4.3.2 Tampering

Tampering threats—unauthorized modification of data, requests, or logs—were evaluated through input validation, integrity controls, and auditing. Input validation test cases confirmed rejection of disallowed upload types, oversized payloads beyond configured megabyte caps, and safe rendering of script-like strings in text fields without browser execution (INPUT-001 through INPUT-003). SQL injection test cases and the separate penetration assessment verified that login fields, search filters, URL parameters, and sorting parameters do not alter database query structure; all PostgreSQL access uses positional parameter binding ($1, $2, …) rather than string concatenation.

Grade modifications, profile updates, backup operations, and other sensitive writes require authenticated sessions with appropriate roles and, for selected destructive actions, explicit server-side confirmation tokens. Grade changes produce audit records capturing prior and new values (LOG-002). Quiz violation payloads submitted by the Quiz Session Guard are stored as structured JSON in submission records for faculty review. A documented gap remains: audit log rows can be deleted by super-admin accounts, and no cryptographic hash chain protects log integrity (LOG-003, Pending Implementation). Tampering of encrypted student PII at rest is mitigated by AES-256-GCM for six designated columns when AES_256_SECRET_KEY is configured.

4.3.3 Repudiation

Repudiation threats—denying that an action occurred—were addressed primarily through monitoring and audit subsystems. Login success, login failure, account lockout, password reset requests, terms acceptance, quiz submission, and unauthorized API access attempts were observed in LMS activity logs and audit tables during testing. The detection rate for expected login and quiz-related events during live API flow verification was 100% for the event types exercised (LOGIN_FAILED, USER_SIGNED_IN, AUTH_LOCKOUT, QUIZ_SUBMITTED hooks, and twenty distinct audit event types present in the database sample).

Monitoring Records and audit statistics views allow administrators to review who performed an action, when it occurred, and contextual payload metadata stripped of secrets. Faculty can inspect per-student quiz violation histories, supporting academic integrity review. Limitations include the absence of legally non-repudiable log storage, reliance on server clock accuracy, and incomplete logging of read-only data access. These constraints mean LenLearn supports administrative accountability and incident review within the capstone scope but does not provide forensic-grade non-repudiation comparable to dedicated SIEM or blockchain-backed audit systems described in enterprise security literature.

4.3.4 Information Disclosure

Information disclosure threats—exposing sensitive data to unauthorized parties—were tested across authentication responses, API authorization, encryption, transport security, and error handling. Passwords are stored as bcrypt hashes with cost factor 12; automated database probes confirmed hash prefixes of $2b$ rather than plaintext (DATA-003). Selected student PII columns stored with enc:v1: prefixes when encryption is enabled (DATA-001). API responses omit password fields, and frontend utilities strip secrets from localStorage snapshots.

Production deployment delivers traffic over HTTPS with valid TLS certificates via Railway and Cloudflare; session cookies use the Secure flag in production (DATA-002). Helmet configures Content-Security-Policy, X-Content-Type-Options, X-Frame-Options, and HSTS in production (CONFIG-002). Generic client error messages replace stack traces in production builds, and debug endpoints under /api/debug/* are disabled (CONFIG-003, verified in staging). SQL injection testing confirmed that failed authentication and malicious inputs do not leak PostgreSQL error strings, table names, or query syntax.

Partially verified and residual disclosure risks include: encryption applied to six student columns only rather than all personally identifiable fields; faculty roster photos and academic metadata remaining unencrypted at rest; and verbose development-mode logging that must remain disabled in production. Cross-origin reflection probes showed no script injection in API responses for tested endpoints. Together, these results indicate effective baseline confidentiality controls with scope-limited encryption coverage.

4.3.5 Denial of Service

Denial-of-service threats— disrupting availability through resource exhaustion—were assessed within ethical capstone boundaries without destructive load testing. Implemented compensating controls include Express body size limits (default ten megabytes), upload size caps per content type, tiered API rate limiting (fifty to one thousand requests per fifteen minutes depending on method and environment), sign-in rate limiting, and account lockout that slows brute-force attacks.

Health check endpoint GET /health returned HTTP 200 during automated probes, confirming basic availability monitoring. Originality checker web-source fetching limits concurrent external requests to eight pages per analysis, bounding outbound network amplification. Full stress testing, slowloris simulation, and distributed denial-of-service scenarios were out of scope per the SQL injection assessment rules of engagement and academic ethics guidelines in Section 3.9. Consequently, DoS results demonstrate control presence and configuration rather than proven resilience under attack-scale traffic.

4.3.6 Elevation of Privilege

Elevation-of-privilege threats— gaining capabilities beyond assigned role—were evaluated through RBAC test cases and static route-guard analysis. Anonymous users cannot access protected student API routes (401). Student sessions attempting administrator list endpoints are denied (403) when live session cookies are supplied in manual tests. Frontend route guards redirect students from /teacher/* and /admin/* paths to the student dashboard, faculty from administrator and student portals, and administrators from faculty and student portals, consistent with the static guard matrix in the RBAC evidence report.

Server-side gates in server/lib/security.js constitute the authoritative authorization boundary; UI redirects alone are insufficient and were validated together with API status codes. Users cannot self-update role, lockout state, or two-factor fields through profile endpoints. Super-administrative privileges are restricted to an email allowlist for destructive audit operations. Documented gaps include the intentionally unauthenticated faculty bootstrap state endpoint and absence of concurrent session revocation, which could allow prolonged use of a stolen session until cookie expiry (seven-day server lifetime versus thirty-minute client idle logout).

4.3.7 Browser-Based Quiz Restriction Results

Browser-based restriction was evaluated as a distinct security domain aligned with the study title. Three test cases verified that (1) entering a secured quiz activates fullscreen mode through the Quiz Session Guard, (2) tab-switch and visibility-change events generate violation records with timestamps and question numbers, and (3) faculty can retrieve violation counts from submission views. Violation types tab_switch and fullscreen_exit are deduplicated within a five-hundred-millisecond window to reduce noise while preserving evidence of deliberate navigation away from the assessment surface.

During student evaluation sessions, simulated tab switches during timed quizzes produced visible violation badges on faculty submission review screens and persisted JSON arrays in quiz_submissions.violations. Cross-tab locks prevented the same student from opening a second active quiz tab in standard browser tests. These results confirm that browser-based restriction operates as an integrity aid rather than absolute proctoring: it increases deterrence and auditability but cannot prevent all forms of academic misconduct outside the browser, consistent with limitations reported in the literature on client-side exam monitoring.

4.3.8 SQL Injection Penetration Test Results

Complementing structured test cases SQLI-001 through SQLI-003, an authorized white-box SQL injection assessment evaluated five critical input vectors: login credential fields, student and faculty search, profile editing forms, URL path parameters, and record filtering or sorting parameters. All five test cases passed with zero vulnerabilities identified, yielding a 100% pass rate. Observed secure behaviors included generic 401 authentication failures, absence of PostgreSQL error leakage, consistent response timing, and parameterized query patterns verified through source review and Burp Suite interception. This result supports the tampering and information disclosure findings above and aligns with OWASP Testing Guide OTG-INPVAL-005 expectations for injection resistance.

4.4 AI-Powered Originality Checker Performance Results

The AI-Powered Originality Checker was evaluated through teacher questionnaire items in Section 4.2.1 (AI plagiarism checker functionality, weighted mean = 5.00, Strongly Agree), controlled text samples with known ground truth, and automated unit tests for plagiarism and AI detection engines. Teacher respondents rated the plagiarism checker as fully meeting their needs during the guided evaluation session.

Controlled analysis used twelve prepared samples: four human-written essays, four AI-generated passages, and four paraphrased or reference-matched submissions. Plagiarism similarity analysis correctly assigned High risk to three of four reference-matched samples (similarity ≥ 71%), Medium risk to the paraphrased sample with partial overlap, and Low risk to human originals without matched sources. AI content detection correctly classified three of four AI-generated samples as Likely AI-generated and three of four human samples as Likely Human, with one mixed-verdict borderline case. Overall classification accuracy was 83.3%, computed as (10 correct ÷ 12 samples) × 100 per Figure 23.

Automated development tests for plagiarismEngine, plagiarismAiEngine, and aiContentDetector modules passed all asserted cases, including risk-band mapping, identical-text similarity detection, and higher AI probability for uniform repetitive text compared to varied human-like writing. Live engine probes during API flow testing reported High risk scores for intentionally copied reference text. These quantitative and automated outcomes indicate that the originality checker meets capstone performance expectations as a faculty decision-support tool, not as an autonomous disciplinary authority.

4.5 Discussion of Findings

4.5.1 Interpretation of Results

The results presented in Sections 4.1 through 4.4 demonstrate that LenLearn was implemented as a functional, role-aware LMS with integrated monitoring, browser-based quiz restrictions, and an AI-assisted originality checker suitable for controlled deployment at Glendale High School. FURPS evaluation indicates that participants perceived functionality, usability, reliability, and performance at Strongly Agree levels (combined weighted means of 4.47, 4.38, 4.69, and 4.34, respectively) and security at Agree level (combined weighted mean of 4.08). Security received the lowest overall rating among the five FURPS areas, driven primarily by modest session-management scores from student and administrator respondents. These perceptions are consistent with observation notes showing successful completion of representative tasks across all three portals.

Security testing corroborates the survey security ratings with empirical evidence rather than opinion alone. An 88% structured test success rate, combined with a 100% SQL injection assessment pass rate and positive STRIDE coverage for spoofing, tampering, and elevation-of-privilege scenarios, indicates that the security architecture described in Chapter 3 was realized in code and behaves as intended under test conditions. Partial failures and pending items—log tamper protection, in-app HTTPS redirect, full production configuration verification, and server-side session lifetime policy—define a clear hardening roadmap rather than fundamental design failure.

The originality checker results (83.3% controlled classification accuracy) sit within reasonable expectations for a capstone-built tool that combines lexical TF-IDF, local semantic embeddings, optional web search, and heuristic AI detection without proprietary training on institution-specific corpora. Faculty perception scores and correct identification of high-similarity samples support using the module to prioritize human review. Misclassification of borderline human and AI samples confirms literature warnings that automated authorship detection should remain advisory.

4.5.2 Achievement of Study Objectives

The findings are examined below in relation to the general and specific objectives of the study.

Objective 1: Analyze institutional requirements and design a secure LMS architecture for Glendale School. The client interview (Appendix C), functional requirements specification, context diagram, data flow diagrams, ERD, and deployment diagram translated school needs into a modular architecture. Implementation results in Section 4.1 confirm that the designed administrator, faculty, and student processes were realized. This objective is achieved.

Objective 2: Develop LenLearn with monitoring records, AI-powered plagiarism checking, and browser-based quiz restrictions. Monitoring Records, audit logging, the originality checker pipeline, and the Quiz Session Guard were implemented and verified through evaluation sessions and security tests. Browser violation detection achieved a 100% pass rate in structured testing. This objective is achieved, with noted partial implementations in log integrity and encryption scope documented transparently.

Objective 3: Implement security controls aligned with OWASP, NIST, and ISO-oriented best practices. Layered controls—including bcrypt hashing, RBAC, parameterized SQL, input sanitization, rate limiting, Helmet headers, AES-256-GCM for selected PII, and two-factor authentication—were verified through automated and manual tests. Known gaps align with the partial status entries in the security controls inventory. This objective is substantially achieved within capstone scope, acknowledging that complete security is a continuous process rather than a one-time deliverable.

Objective 4: Evaluate system quality and security using FURPS and STRIDE-based vulnerability assessment. Ninety-six questionnaire responses, SUS scores above the industry benchmark, twenty-six structured security tests, STRIDE mapping (Table 4.8), and SQL injection penetration testing provide triangulated evaluation evidence analyzed per Section 3.7.3. This objective is achieved.

Objective 5: Demonstrate LenLearn for academic integrity and secure digital learning at Glendale High School. Guided walkthroughs with one administrator, five faculty members, and ninety students completed successfully with voluntary informed consent. Participants operated role-appropriate modules in a controlled setting. This objective is achieved for pilot demonstration; longitudinal school-wide adoption was outside the capstone timeline.

4.5.3 Practical Significance

Practically, LenLearn offers Glendale High School a dedicated alternative to generic commercial LMS platforms by embedding school-specific workflows—section management, curriculum guides, localized branding, and integrated originality review—within a single tenant-controlled system. Monitoring Records give administrators visibility into authentication anomalies and grade changes without requiring a separate security information product. Browser-based quiz restrictions provide a lightweight integrity mechanism compatible with standard computer-laboratory browsers, avoiding the cost and privacy concerns of third-party proctoring services cited in client consultation responses.

For faculty, the originality checker reduces manual web searching by aggregating lexical and semantic signals with optional AI authorship hints, supporting faster identification of submissions that warrant closer reading. For students, centralized access to materials, quizzes, and grades on a consistent portal may reduce fragmentation across informal channels previously used by the school. Deployment on Railway with Cloudflare demonstrates a feasible path from capstone prototype to hosted service without on-premises server maintenance burdens appropriate to a medium-sized private school context.

4.5.4 Technical Significance

Technically, LenLearn contributes a reference implementation of security-by-design patterns in a modern JavaScript stack: Better Auth integration with institute-specific lockout and portal enforcement, dual-layer plagiarism analysis combining classical IR techniques with local transformer embeddings, client-side quiz guards coordinated with server-side violation persistence, and selective field-level encryption in PostgreSQL without ORM abstraction. The 98.4% automated test pass rate and documented security evidence scripts (npm run security:evidence) provide reproducible artifacts uncommon in undergraduate projects.

The STRIDE-oriented assessment explicitly links threats to controls and gaps, supporting risk-based prioritization of future work—cryptographic audit chaining, expanded PII encryption, server-side session idle revocation, magic-byte upload validation, and formal load testing. The SQL injection report’s 100% pass rate across five vectors validates the architectural decision to use parameterized queries throughout, a lesson transferable to other PostgreSQL-backed education systems.

4.5.5 Academic Significance

Academically, this study extends cybersecurity capstone literature by integrating software engineering delivery with structured security evaluation rather than treating security as a post-hoc checklist. It demonstrates how FURPS perceptual metrics and STRIDE technical threat modeling can complement each other: users may strongly agree that a system “feels secure” while technical tests still reveal partial implementations: a finding with pedagogical value for future BSIT Cybersecurity students.

The controlled originality checker evaluation illustrates responsible claims-making for AI in education—reporting accuracy on labeled samples, acknowledging heuristic limits, and positioning automated scores as advisory inputs to faculty judgment. This aligns with emerging academic integrity frameworks that emphasize human oversight of algorithmic tools. Documented ethical procedures (Section 3.9), data minimization, and consent agreements further model how institution-facing capstone projects can comply with the Data Privacy Act of 2012 and school policies while conducting live system evaluation.

4.5.6 Limitations and Recommendations

Several limitations qualify the generalizability of these findings. Evaluation occurred primarily in development and pilot demonstration settings rather than a full academic year of production use. Security testing excluded destructive denial-of-service experiments and third-party certification. AI evaluation used twelve controlled samples and does not represent all generative-AI writing styles or cross-language plagiarism. Survey and SUS scores reflect short exposure sessions and may shift with extended daily use. Server session cookies remain valid for seven days despite thirty-minute client idle logout, creating a policy gap that should be closed before high-stakes examination periods.

Based on the results, the researchers recommend the following for future iterations: implement cryptographic audit-log integrity or append-only storage; extend AES-256-GCM encryption to all PII fields; add server-side idle session revocation aligned with client timeout; complete magic-byte validation on uploads; conduct formal load testing before school-wide rollout; retrain or calibrate AI detection with institution-specific writing samples; and schedule periodic penetration retesting as modules evolve.

4.5.7 Summary

In summary, LenLearn fulfills the capstone intent of delivering a secure, monitoring-capable LMS with AI-assisted originality review and browser-based quiz restrictions for Glendale School. FURPS evaluation shows Strongly Agree perceived quality for functionality (4.47), usability (4.38), reliability (4.69), and performance (4.34), and Agree perceived quality for security (4.08). STRIDE-based security assessment confirms effective coverage of major threat categories with documented partial gaps. The originality checker performs adequately on controlled samples as a faculty support tool. The study objectives are achieved or substantially achieved within defined scope and limitations, yielding practical value for the client institution and technical and academic contributions to secure educational systems development.
