CHAPTER 6
RECOMMENDATIONS

This chapter presents recommendations derived from the findings of the LenLearn capstone study. The recommendations are grounded in FURPS-based system quality evaluation (Section 4.2), STRIDE-oriented security testing and vulnerability assessment (Section 4.3), AI-Powered Originality Checker performance results (Section 4.4), and the limitations discussed in Section 4.5.6. They are intended to guide future development, research, and institutional deployment of LenLearn: A Secure Learning Management System with AI-Powered Plagiarism Checker for Glendale School without overstating what the evaluation already demonstrated.

6.1 System Enhancements

Although LenLearn received Strongly Agree combined weighted means for functionality (4.47), usability (4.38), reliability (4.69), and performance (4.34) in Section 4.2, several item-level scores indicate areas where targeted module and interface improvements may support daily use before wider adoption. Students rated PDF uploads finishing within a reasonable time at a weighted mean of 4.18 (Agree) in Section 4.2.4, which was lower than quiz loading without long delays at weighted mean 4.46 (Strongly Agree). Students also rated submitting PDF files for assignments and activities at a weighted mean of 4.16 (Agree) under usability in Section 4.2.2. The sole administrator respondent rated handling large numbers of student and faculty records well at a weighted mean of 3.00 (Neutral) under performance in Section 4.2.4 and data not being lost after backup and restore operations at a weighted mean of 3.00 (Neutral) under reliability in Section 4.2.3. Because only one administrator participated (n = 1), these scores cannot be generalized broadly; however, they suggest that upload feedback, scalability, and restore confidence were not fully established during the capstone pilot.

It is recommended that future iterations add visible upload progress indicators, pre-upload file-size guidance aligned with configured server limits, and clearer success confirmation after PDF submission to address the comparatively lower student upload and usability scores in Sections 4.2.2 and 4.2.4. It is recommended that Glendale School administration prepare role-specific administrator onboarding materials, inline help text for first-time users of Audit Logs, Data Backup, and Archive Vault modules, and a short guided checklist before assigning backup or restore tasks to new staff, in response to the administrator learning-curve item at weighted mean 4.00 (Agree) in Section 4.2.2 while other administrator usability items received 5.00 (Strongly Agree). These enhancements should be undertaken on a short-term basis before school-wide deployment and should not be presented as guaranteed performance improvements until validated under production loads.

It is recommended that future work include database indexing review, paginated admin list views, formal load testing at Glendale School enrollment volumes, scheduled backup-and-restore verification drills, and post-restore integrity checks with documented outcomes in the Data Backup and Archive Vault modules. It is recommended that follow-up evaluation with additional administrator respondents be conducted before treating scalability and restore confidence as institute-wide conclusions. The development team and school IT staff should carry out these automation and scalability tasks on a medium-term schedule during the first semester of pilot operation.

6.2 Security Improvements

Security received the lowest combined FURPS weighted mean among the five quality areas at 4.08 (Agree) in Section 4.2.5. Students rated automatic logout after inactivity at a weighted mean of 3.47 (Agree), the lowest student security score, and account lockout after repeated wrong passwords at a weighted mean of 3.74 (Agree). Teachers rated email login codes favorably at weighted mean 4.65 (Strongly Agree) in Section 4.2.5, while the sole administrator rated automatic logout after a period of inactivity at a weighted mean of 3.00 (Neutral). Technical security testing in Section 4.3 reported an overall structured test success rate of 88.0%, with pending or partially verified items including cryptographic audit-log integrity (LOG-003), scope-limited AES-256-GCM encryption for selected student personally identifiable information fields, and a policy gap between thirty-minute client idle logout and a longer server-side session cookie lifetime noted in Section 4.5.6. These findings indicate that authentication and monitoring controls are present in the system design described in Section 4.1 but were not consistently perceived during short supervised evaluation sessions and remain partially implemented in code.

It is recommended that a WAF (Web Application Firewall, a filter that blocks malicious web traffic) be formally configured and reviewed on the existing Cloudflare edge protection already used in front of glendalehs-lms.com as described in Section 4.1. It is recommended that improved MFA be evaluated on a long-term basis after email one-time-password authentication is stable in production, including WebAuthn/FIDO2 passkeys, hardware security keys, or risk-based step-up verification aligned with NIST digital identity guidance, without implying that the current email MFA failed the capstone evaluation given teacher OTP ratings at weighted mean 4.65 (Strongly Agree). It is recommended that login failures, account lockouts, and unauthorized access attempts recorded in Monitoring Records be routed to a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management, a centralized system for reviewing security alerts) or an equivalent centralized alerting workflow. It is recommended that existing rate limiting on sign-in, OTP, and general API endpoints described in Section 4.1 be reviewed and tuned under production traffic rather than treated as fixed at development defaults. It is recommended that the student portal display a visible idle-session warning before the thirty-minute client logout takes effect and present a clear, generic lockout message with cooldown guidance when account lockout activates after failed login attempts, on a short-term basis before deployment.

It is recommended that a secure SDLC (secure software development lifecycle, security practices integrated into design, coding, and release) include dependency and container image scanning, structured code review for authentication and role-based access control changes, and completion of magic-byte validation on uploaded files before each release. It is recommended that periodic VA/PT (vulnerability assessment and penetration testing, structured security retesting by qualified assessors) be scheduled after major module changes, subject to institutional approval and ethical rules of engagement as noted in Section 4.5.6. It is recommended that future iterations prioritize append-only or cryptographically protected audit storage, extend AES-256-GCM encryption to all designated personally identifiable information columns, and implement centralized server-side session revocation aligned with the client idle timeout, with re-authentication required before destructive administrator actions such as backup restore, archive deletion, or audit-log management. These measures constitute a risk-based hardening roadmap; they do not claim to make LenLearn fully tamper-proof or comprehensively encrypted and should be validated through retesting rather than assumed complete upon implementation.

6.3 Future Research

Future research should address sample-size limitations that constrain generalizability of the FURPS findings in Section 4.2. The post-test evaluation included ninety students, five teachers, and one administrator, and evaluation occurred through a single guided walkthrough as described in Section 3.8.4 rather than a full academic term. Teacher and administrator subsamples are particularly small, and administrator item scores reflect a single respondent (n = 1). The capstone evaluation was also conducted in a controlled, supervised setting at Glendale High School, which may not fully reflect unsupervised classroom behavior, peak-hour network congestion, or end-of-term grading workloads discussed in Section 4.5.6.

It is recommended that subsequent studies enroll a larger and more representative set of faculty and administrator participants and conduct evaluation across a full academic term rather than a single supervised session. It is recommended that future research include classroom-based use without continuous researcher facilitation and measure system behavior during enrollment periods and examination weeks with incident reports collected from teachers and students operating LenLearn as a primary daily tool. Glendale School administration and future researchers should plan these longitudinal studies on a long-term basis after initial deployment, as such evidence may reveal whether lower scores on PDF upload speed, session timeout awareness, and backup-and-restore confidence persist or change with familiarity.

The AI-Powered Originality Checker was assessed using twelve controlled samples with 83.3% overall classification accuracy in Section 4.4 and was not evaluated on anonymized real student submissions across diverse writing styles, languages, or paraphrase patterns. Security research in Section 4.3 did not include destructive denial-of-service testing or third-party certification. It is recommended that future research test the originality checker against de-identified institutional submissions with faculty-established ground truth and report false-positive and false-negative rates by assignment type. It is recommended that formal load testing and independent VA/PT precede any school-wide rollout. It is recommended that comparative studies evaluate whether WebAuthn passkeys, hardware security keys, or optional biometric step-up authentication improve perceived and measured security outcomes relative to email one-time passwords alone, using questionnaires under Republic Act No. 10173–compliant consent procedures before any biometric data collection at Glendale School. These directions are long-term research responsibilities for future researchers and qualified security assessors.

6.4 Deployment Advice

FURPS scores in Section 4.2 reflect a controlled pilot, not a complete academic year of production operation; immediate institute-wide replacement of existing teaching workflows would therefore carry greater uncertainty than a phased approach. Production deployment described in Section 4.1 used Railway hosting with Cloudflare edge protection and PostgreSQL database services at glendalehs-lms.com. It is recommended that Glendale School administration deploy LenLearn in phases rather than through immediate institute-wide adoption, beginning with selected grade levels or sections, designating an internal system owner responsible for user provisioning and incident escalation, and maintaining documented rollback and communication procedures if critical issues arise during expansion. It is recommended that hosting remain on the Railway platform already evaluated in Section 4.1, the production domain glendalehs-lms.com be maintained with documented DNS ownership, and valid SSL certificates (TLS encryption for web traffic) be verified on renewal so that all user traffic continues to be served over HTTPS.

It is recommended that automated backups from the Data Backup module be scheduled with off-site retention and tested restore drills to address the administrator backup-and-restore reliability score at weighted mean 3.00 (Neutral) in Section 4.2.3. It is recommended that system monitoring include health check review, uptime alerting, and routine inspection of Monitoring Records after deployment so that authentication anomalies and grade-related events remain visible to authorized administrators. It is recommended that production hardening steps be verified before each expansion phase, including disabling debug endpoints under production configuration, enforcing Secure session cookie flags, restricting environment secrets to authorized personnel, and confirming Helmet HTTP security headers described in Section 4.1. These operational measures should be completed on a short-term to medium-term basis during the first semester of pilot operation.

Production deployment must continue to align with Republic Act No. 10173, the Data Privacy Act of 2012, as observed during capstone data gathering in Section 3.9. It is recommended that Glendale School administration define a data retention and deletion schedule for student and faculty records, assign institutional accountability for privacy compliance to a designated school representative, and verify reliable email delivery for one-time passwords on school networks before rollout. Teachers rated assignment management, the AI plagiarism checker, and grade-related functions at Strongly Agree levels in Section 4.2.1, and these strengths may be leveraged during faculty orientation. It is recommended that student orientation emphasize PDF assignment submission and timed quiz procedures, which recorded comparatively lower performance and usability scores including PDF upload speed at weighted mean 4.18 (Agree) in Section 4.2.4 and session-timeout awareness at weighted mean 3.47 (Agree) in Section 4.2.5. It is recommended that stronger authentication rollout begin with administrator and faculty accounts if adopted later, that updated privacy notices explain how authentication data are stored and used, and that optional biometric enrollment for minor students be deferred until institutional policy and parental consent procedures are formally established.

6.5 Summary of Recommendations

In summary, the recommendations in this chapter respond directly to evidence from Chapter 4 rather than to assumed deficiencies in LenLearn as a whole. Section 6.1 proposes targeted system enhancements—upload feedback, administrator onboarding support, database and backup verification, and load testing—to address item-level FURPS gaps such as student PDF upload scores at weighted means 4.18 and 4.16 and administrator scalability and restore confidence scores at weighted mean 3.00 (n = 1). Section 6.2 proposes layered security improvements, including WAF configuration, improved MFA evaluation, SIEM-style centralized alerting, rate limiting review, secure SDLC practices, periodic VA/PT, audit storage hardening, and session-management alignment, grounded in the combined security weighted mean of 4.08 (Agree), the 88.0% structured test success rate in Section 4.3, and documented partial implementations in Section 4.5.6. Section 6.3 proposes future research with larger faculty and administrator samples, full-semester longitudinal use, de-identified originality-checker validation beyond 83.3% accuracy on twelve controlled samples, and independent security retesting. Section 6.4 proposes phased deployment on Railway with maintained domain and SSL certificate management, automated backups, system monitoring, production hardening, RA 10173–aligned privacy practices, and user orientation for PDF and quiz workflows. None of these recommendations claim that LenLearn is fully secure, perfectly scalable, or ready for unconditional institute-wide adoption; they define a practical roadmap for Glendale School and future developers to strengthen, validate, and deploy the system within the scope demonstrated by the capstone evaluation.
